The Modern Meal: Sustenance through Ritual

April 7–August 6, 2017, The Mildred Lane Kemper Art Museum

Our human need to eat is universal; as a culture, however, we hunger for something beyond food itself. The Modern Meal: Sustenance through Ritual examines visual representations of the meal and its role as a ritual of consumption connected to location, resources, and social organization. Historically mealtimes have been determined by the workday, and dramatic changes in the workplace—such as fixed work shifts in factories or offices and, more recently, constant communication in the global information economy—have contributed to transitions in when, how, and what we eat. In their portrayals of the meal, modern artists have reflected on these changes and probed themes of social equality, identity, globalization, and food availability. Many of the artworks included in this exhibition express the tension between real and imagined meal spaces, while others reveal the effects that labor, marginalization, and war can have on the very character of the meal. The Modern Meal highlights the spectrum of experiences, individual and shared, that are explored across diverse artistic depictions of the meal. To structure to the exhibition, we divided it into three sections, each a dualism. These were inspired by the artist, author, and activist Allan Sekula, who has theorized that “labor is always shadowed by the absence of labor.”

Sekula here references the ways in which propagandistic images that celebrate labor as an essential part of a prosperous culture simultaneously imply “the shadow”—the threat (or promise) of a society without work. “Place | Displacement” illustrates how the spaces we occupy govern our daily routines by emphasizing the mealtime rituals of those displaced from their homes, either physically or psychologically. “Plenitude | Scarcity” considers not only the apportioning of food, but also the ways in which interactions between those at the table manifest power dynamics and degrees of privilege. “Freedom | Regimentation” addresses the roles of gender and labor in organizing meal times and spaces. By exploring the consumer, the setting, and the food in proximity, these three sections suggest the complexity of this apparently simple social form. The Modern Meal: Sustenance through Ritual examines how the meal’s temporal, spatial, and social qualities are as important as the food in determining what constitutes the meal. There has been a long history of food in visual art, from the religious iconography of Leonardo da Vinci’s The Last Supper to the references to consumer culture in Andy Warhol’s Campbell’s Soup Cans A variety of art exhibitions in the past decade have addressed food and meals, including examinations of the kitchen as a site of social production and the meal as a performative exchange between host and diner. In contrast to these approaches, we seek to consider the meal itself as a ritualized performance in order to understand the kind of sustenance we seek from shared meals. This spectrum of experiences at the table can in turn expose the underlying politics of the meal, on an individual, interpersonal, and global scale.
